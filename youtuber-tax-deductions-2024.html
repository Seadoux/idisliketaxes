Introduction

If you're making money on YouTube, you're running a business. And that means you can deduct business expenses on your taxes. But most YouTubers miss thousands of dollars in deductions simply because they don't know what qualifies.

In this guide, we'll cover everything you can write off as a YouTube creator, from obvious expenses like cameras to surprising ones like VidCon tickets and your internet bill.

Filming Equipment Deductions

Any equipment you use to create videos is 100% deductible. This includes:

  • Cameras: DSLRs, mirrorless cameras, webcams, action cameras
  • Lenses: Wide-angle, telephoto, macro lenses
  • Lighting: Ring lights, softboxes, LED panels, backdrops
  • Audio: Microphones, audio interfaces, boom poles, pop filters
  • Stabilization: Tripods, gimbals, sliders, monopods
  • Accessories: SD cards, batteries, cables, camera bags

Important: Equipment over $2,500 may need to be depreciated over several years rather than deducted all at once. Use Section 179 to deduct the full amount immediately (up to certain limits).

Software & Subscriptions

Every software subscription you use for YouTube is deductible:

  • Editing software: Adobe Creative Cloud, Final Cut Pro, DaVinci Resolve Studio
  • YouTube tools: TubeBuddy, VidIQ, Social Blade Pro
  • Music licensing: Epidemic Sound, Artlist, Soundstripe
  • Stock footage: Storyblocks, Artgrid
  • Graphics tools: Canva Pro, Photoshop
  • Collaboration: Dropbox, Google Workspace, Slack

Travel & Events

If you travel to create content or attend creator events, it's deductible:

  • VidCon, VidSummit, Playlist Live: 100% deductible (tickets, hotel, flights)
  • Content creation trips: Travel to locations for videos
  • Collaboration meetings: Flying to meet other creators
  • Industry events: Social Media Marketing World, etc.

Pro tip: Keep detailed records of the business purpose for each trip. Take photos, save itineraries, document what videos came from the trip.

Home Office Deduction

If you film, edit, or manage your channel from home, you can deduct a portion of:

  • Rent or mortgage interest
  • Utilities (electricity, water, gas)
  • Internet (typically 50% business use)
  • Renters or homeowners insurance
  • Repairs and maintenance

Calculate the percentage by dividing your studio space square footage by your total home square footage. If your studio is 150 sq ft and your home is 1,000 sq ft, you can deduct 15% of eligible expenses.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Not tracking business meals: Coffee meetings with other creators or editors? 50% deductible.

2. Mixing personal and business expenses: Use a separate credit card for YouTube purchases.

3. Forgetting about freelancer payments: If you pay editors, thumbnail designers, or VAs over $600/year, you need to issue them a 1099-NEC.

4. Not keeping receipts: The IRS requires documentation. Save receipts digitally.

5. Deducting 100% of mixed-use expenses: Phone and internet should be 50% unless you have a dedicated business line.
